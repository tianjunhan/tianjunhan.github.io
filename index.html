Research

I am a fluid dynamicist focusing on underwater propulsion. I employ hydrofoils and fish-like robots to reveal scaling laws that predict thrust, power consumption, maneuverability, and stable formations in bio-inspired propulsion. My research provides guidance for the development and control of advanced underwater bio-inspired vehicles.

My thoughts on scaling laws: I am always fascinated by the beauty and power of scaling laws in uncovering fundamental physics and solving engineering problems. Scaling laws reveal the dominant factors governing physical phenomena. Moreover, due to their simplicity, they are readily applicable to real-world problems and can be effectively used for large-scale training of AI models.

The distinctions between scaling laws and curve fitting: 1. Scaling laws are derived from physical principles, such as Newton’s second law, the Kutta-Joukowski theorem, and the Euler-Bernoulli beam equation. 2. In scaling laws, units are consistent between the left- and right-hand sides, as well as among all terms on the right-hand side. 3. Each term on the right-hand side has a clear physical meaning. 4. Scaling laws’ validity is independent of datasets. 5. Scaling laws are valid in asymmpotic conditions. For example, a scaling law that predicts the thrust of oscillating hydrofoils should yield zero thrust when the amplitude or frequency becomes zero. In contrast, curve fitting does not satisfy all of these conditions simultaneously.

Here are the highlights of selected completed research projects:

Tailoring the bending pattern of non-uniformly flexible pitching hydrofoils enhances propulsive efficiency

We found that the propulsive efficiency of flexible pitching hydrofoils can be increased by more than 70% by simply tailoring their bending patterns.

We presented a scaling law that predicts the resonant condition of non-uniformly flexible hydrofoils from their frequencies, bending stiffness, and flexion ratios:
\[\Pi_{k} = f(f,\, EI, \,\lambda)\].

Revealing the mechanism and scaling laws behind equilibrium altitudes of near-ground pitching hydrofoils

We found that the equilibrium altitude in unsteady ground effect is generated by a balance between positive wake-induced lift and negative quasi-steady lift while the added mass lift does not play a role.

We presented a scaling law that predicts the equilibrium altitude of pitching hydrofoils from their Strouhal numbers and reduced frequencies:
\[D^* = f(St,\, k)\].

Tailoring formations of self-organising hydrofoil schools towards high-efficiency

We found that in-line stable formations scale with the actual wake wavelength of the leader, rather than the nominal wake wavelength that is the ratio of velocity over oscillating frequency. Leveraging mismatched amplitudes, unconstrained hydrofoils can settle into the high-efficiency zone identified by previous constrained studies of in-line schooling.

We presented a scaling law that predicts the actual wake wavelength of the leader from its Strouhal number and reduced frequency:
\[\lambda = f(St,\, k)\].

Development of a turning control strategy for a bio-inspired underwater vehicle

We developed a single-actuator turning strategy for the Tunabot by applying a frequency asymmetry between the left and right half strokes of one tail-beat cycle.

We presented a scaling law that predicts the turning radius of the Tunabot from its base frequency and frequency asymmetry:
\[r = f(f,\, \Delta f)\].

Here are the highlights of selected ongoing research projects:

Stale formations and Energetics of a schooling bio-robots

We are examing the energetics of a bio-robot school that consist of two independently free-swimming tuna-like robots.

We are developing a scaling law that predicts the cost of transport of the school from its lateral spacing, tail-beat frequency and amplitude, and phase synchrony:
\[CoT = f(Y^*,\, f,\,A,\,\phi)\].

Vertical manuever of the Tunabot using pectoral fins

We are developing a vertical manuever using pectroal fins that enable the Tunabot to climb and dive without introducing additional pitch angle or angular velocity.

We are developing a scaling law that predicts the vertical trajectory of the Tunabot from its pectroal-fin pitch angle and tail-beat frequency:
\[H^* = f(\theta,\, f)\].

Scaling the propulsive performance of large hydrofoil schools

We are investigating the performance of pitching hydrofoil schools of more than ten swimmers.

We are developing a scaling law that predicts the thrust enhancement of hydrofoils from their vertical and lateral spacings,Strouhal number and reduced frequency:
\[T^* = f(X^*,\,Y^*,\, St,\,f)\].
